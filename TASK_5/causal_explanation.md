# Causal Graph Explanation (from "causal_graph.py")

## Causal Variables

### "shots"

- [shots -> TVD]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "shots" and "TVD," where "shots" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "TVD." This edge exists because quantum measurement is inherently probabilistic, so a small number of repeated runs produces a measured distribution that only loosely approximates the true underlying probabilities; running the circuit more times averages out that sampling noise and pulls the measured distribution closer to the ideal one. Since "TVD" measures the absolute value distance between an ideal probability and the measured probability, more "shots" means the measured probability gets closer to the ideal probability, lowering "TVD" (an improvement in output quality). However, there is a diminishing returns curve, because, based on the "TVD Stability by Shot Count" graph, past ~2000 shots, increasing shots doesn't decrease the TVD as much. The direction only points from "shots" to "TVD," and not the other way, because "shots" is a configuration value chosen before the circuit is even run, while "TVD" is only computed afterward, from the measurement results. Since "TVD" doesn't exist yet at the moment "shots" is set, it cannot be the one influencing "shots."

### "noise_rate"

- [noise_rate -> TVD]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "noise_rate" and "TVD," where "noise_rate" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "TVD." This edge exists because environmental interference and hardware flaws physically perturb qubit states and gate operations during execution, pushing the actual measured outcomes away from what the ideal, noiseless circuit would have produced. Based on the "TVD by Noise Rate" graph, as you increase the "noise_rate," the total variation distance (or "TVD") becomes higher (a worsening of output quality), since "TVD" measures the absolute value distance between an ideal probability and the measured probability, and increasing "noise_rate" means the measured probability gets farther away from the ideal probability. The direction only points from "noise_rate" to "TVD," and not the other way, because "noise_rate" is a configuration value chosen before the circuit is even run, while "TVD" is only computed afterward, from the measurement results. Since "TVD" doesn't exist yet at the moment "noise_rate" is set, it cannot be the one influencing "noise_rate."

- [noise_rate -> success_probability]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "noise_rate" and "success_probability," where "noise_rate" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "success_probability." This edge exists because environmental interference and hardware flaws degrade the qubit states and entangling operations that Grover's amplitude amplification depends on, making it harder for the marked state's probability to stay boosted above the noise floor by the time measurement happens. Based on the "Grover: Success Probability by Noise Rate" graph, as you increase the "noise_rate," "success_probability" (which measures the probability that a circuit will output an expected output, and is only applicable to the Grover circuit) lowers. The direction only points from "noise_rate" to "success_probability," and not the other way, because "noise_rate" is a configuration value chosen before the circuit is even run, while "success_probability" is only computed afterward, from the measurement results. Since "success_probability" doesn't exist yet at the moment "noise_rate" is set, it cannot be the one influencing "noise_rate."

### "optimization_level"

- [optimization_level -> depth]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "optimization_level" and "depth," where "optimization_level" is a variable that affects the mediator metric "depth." This edge exists because "optimization_level" is a parameter (from 0 to 3) controlling how much computational effort the transpiler spends compressing and restructuring a quantum circuit, which in-turn influences the "depth" of the circuit, since "depth" is one of the factors Qiskit considers. Based on the "Transpiled Depth by Optimization Level" graph, as you increase the "optimization_level," the "transpiled_depth" (which is the resulting horizontal length of the quantum circuit) lowers noticeably for the Grover circuit, while staying essentially flat for the other four circuit types, meaning the strength of this effect depends heavily on which circuit is being run. This makes sense, since increasing "optimization_level" only helps when there is actually room for the transpiler to compress the circuit further; some circuits may already be at or near their minimal depth. The direction only points from "optimization_level" to "depth," and not the other way, because "optimization_level" is a configuration value preemptively chosen before the circuit is even run, while "depth" is only known afterward, once the transpiler has processed the circuit. Since the final "depth" doesn't exist yet at the moment "optimization_level" is set, it cannot be the one influencing "optimization_level."

- [optimization_level -> count_2q]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "optimization_level" and "count_2q," where "optimization_level" is a variable that affects the mediator metric "count_2q." This edge exists because, by definition, "optimization_level" is a parameter (from 0 to 3) controlling how much computational effort the transpiler spends compressing and restructuring a quantum circuit to minimize gate count and circuit depth, including the count of two-qubit gates, before running it on a quantum device. However, based on the "Two-Qubit Gate Count by Optimization Level" graph, this effect did not appear in this dataset; every circuit type shows a perfectly flat "count_2q" across all optimization levels, including Grover, which did show a depth reduction in the earlier graph. This is likely because the five circuits tested (2-5 qubits, relatively simple structures) did not contain redundant or cancellable two-qubit gates for the transpiler to act on; the mechanism for this edge is real in principle, since Qiskit's transpiler is documented to perform two-qubit gate cancellation/merging at higher optimization levels, but it may only activate for larger or more complex circuits than the ones tested here. The direction only points from "optimization_level" to "count_2q," and not the other way, because "optimization_level" is a configuration value preemptively chosen before the circuit is even run, while "count_2q" is only known afterward, once the transpiler has processed the circuit.

### "circuit_name"

- [circuit_name -> depth]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "circuit_name" and "depth," where "circuit_name" is a variable that affects the mediator metric "depth." This edge exists because each of the five basic circuit types has a fundamentally different gate structure and algorithmic design, so the circuit chosen inherently determines how many sequential gate layers it transpiles to, independent of any other configuration. Based on the "Transpiled Depth by Optimization Level, All Circuits" graph, all five circuit types sit at clearly different, largely flat depth levels regardless of optimization_level. For example, Grover transpiles to a depth around 30, Variable Depth around 22, Parameterized around 4.5, and Bell/GHZ around 3. The direction only points from "circuit_name" to "depth," and not the other way, because "circuit_name" identifies which circuit was selected to run, decided before execution, while "depth" is only known afterward, once the transpiler has processed that specific circuit. Since the final "depth" doesn't exist yet at the moment "circuit_name" is set, it cannot be the one influencing "circuit_name."

- [circuit_name -> count_2q]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "circuit_name" and "count_2q," where "circuit_name" is a variable that affects the mediator metric "count_2q." This edge exists because each of the five basic circuit types is built from a different sequence of entangling operations by design, so the circuit chosen inherently determines how many two-qubit gates it contains, independent of any other configuration. Based on the "Two-Qubit Gate Count by Optimization Level, All Circuits" graph, most of the five circuit types sit at different, largely flat "count_2q" levels regardless of optimization_level. For example, Grover's count_2q is around 14, Variable Depth's count_2q around 15.5, Parameterized/GHZ count_2q around 2.5, and Bell count_2q around 1. The direction only points from "circuit_name" to "count_2q," and not the other way, because "circuit_name" identifies which circuit was selected to run, decided before execution, while "count_2q" is only calculated afterward, once that specific circuit is processed. Since the final "count_2q" doesn't exist yet at the moment "circuit_name" is set, it cannot be the one influencing "circuit_name."

- [circuit_name -> success_probability]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "circuit_name" and "success_probability," where "circuit_name" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "success_probability" directly, not only through the mediators "depth" and "count_2q." This edge exists because "success_probability" is defined specifically for the Grover circuit as the probability that the algorithm's amplitude amplification process successfully boosts the marked state's probability above the noise floor, rather than a generic measure of output quality like "TVD." This is tied to the specific algorithmic structure of Grover's search (its oracle design and number of amplification iterations), not just to how large or gate-heavy the resulting circuit is. Even if two different circuits happened to transpile to the same "depth" and "count_2q," they would not necessarily have the same "success_probability," since that metric depends on whether the specific algorithm being run is one that is even trying to amplify a marked state at all. This is also why "success_probability" is undefined for the other four circuit types (Bell, GHZ, Parameterized, Variable Depth); they are not search algorithms with a "correct answer" to amplify toward, so the metric does not apply to them regardless of their depth or gate count. The direction only points from "circuit_name" to "success_probability," and not the other way, because "circuit_name" identifies which circuit was selected to run, decided before execution, while "success_probability" is only computed afterward, from the measurement results.

### "num_qubits"

- [num_qubits -> depth]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "num_qubits" and "depth," where "num_qubits" is a variable that affects the mediator metric "depth." This edge exists because more qubits generally means a circuit needs more layers of gates to entangle, manipulate, or otherwise involve all of them, so increasing the number of qubits inherently tends to increase how many sequential layers the transpiler produces. Based on the "Transpiled Depth by Number of Qubits" graph, as "num_qubits" increases, "depth" increases as well for the GHZ, Grover, and Parameterized circuits (Bell and Variable Depth were excluded from this comparison since Bell circuit uses a fixed 2-qubit configuration, and you can only modify the depth of a variable-depth circuit through passing in a value). Notably, for the Grover circuit, increasing "num_qubits" from 2 to 4 increases "depth" from around 10 to 57. The direction only points from "num_qubits" to "depth," and not the other way, because "num_qubits" is decided before execution, while "depth" is only known afterward, once the transpiler has processed that specific circuit.

- [num_qubits -> count_2q]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "num_qubits" and "count_2q," where "num_qubits" is a variable that affects the mediator metric "count_2q." This edge exists because more qubits generally means more pairs of qubits available to entangle, so increasing the number of qubits inherently tends to increase how many two-qubit gates a circuit needs to involve all of them. Based on the "Two-Qubit Gate Count by Number of Qubits (All Circuits Except Bell)" graph, as "num_qubits" increases, "count_2q" increases as well for the GHZ, Grover, Parameterized, and Variable Depth circuits (Bell was excluded from this comparison since it uses a fixed 2-qubit configuration with no qubit-count variation to plot). Notably, for the Grover and Variable Depth circuits, increasing "num_qubits" from 2 to 4 increases "count_2q" from around 3 to 28 and 6 to 18, respectively. The direction only points from "num_qubits" to "count_2q," and not the other way, because "num_qubits" is decided before execution, while "count_2q" is only calculated afterward, once the transpiler has processed that specific circuit.

- [num_qubits -> TVD]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "num_qubits" and "TVD," where "num_qubits" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "TVD" directly, not only through the mediators "depth" and "count_2q." This edge exists because every additional qubit carries its own idle and readout error on real and simulated quantum hardware, which accumulates across the whole circuit regardless of depth or gate count. This direct effect was confirmed by grouping the dataset into bins of similar "depth" and "count_2q" values, then comparing "TVD" across different "num_qubits" within the same bin; even when depth and count_2q were held roughly constant, "TVD" still increased as "num_qubits" increased. The direction only points from "num_qubits" to "TVD," and not the other way, because "num_qubits" is decided before execution, while "TVD" is only computed afterward, from the measurement results.

- [num_qubits -> runtime]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "num_qubits" and "runtime," where "num_qubits" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "runtime" directly, not only through the mediators "depth" and "count_2q." This edge exists because simulating a quantum circuit requires representing a state space that grows exponentially with the number of qubits, so a larger qubit count means a larger underlying state vector for the simulator to track and update at every step, independent of how deep or gate-heavy the circuit itself is. Based on the "Runtime by Number of Qubits (All Circuits Except Bell)" graph, "runtime" increases as "num_qubits" increases for the GHZ, Grover, Parameterized, and Variable Depth circuits, with Grover and Variable Depth showing the steepest increase. The direction only points from "num_qubits" to "runtime," and not the other way, because "num_qubits" is decided before execution, while "runtime" is only measured afterward, once the simulation has finished.

## Mediator Variables

### "depth"

- [depth -> runtime]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "depth" and "runtime," where "depth" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "runtime." This edge exists because "depth" represents the number of sequential gate layers in the transpiled circuit, and a simulator must process each layer one after another, in order, to simulate the circuit's evolution, so a deeper circuit requires more sequential computational steps independent of how many qubits or gates exist at each individual step. Based on the "Runtime by Transpiled Depth, All Circuits" graph, as "depth" increases, runtime roughly increases as well; for example, an increase of "depth" from 4 to 76 results in an increase in runtime from about 0.0055 to 0.0103 seconds. The direction only points from "depth" to "runtime," and not the other way, because "depth" is a property of the transpiled circuit, fixed before the circuit is simulated, while "runtime" is only measured afterward, once the simulation has finished.

- [depth -> TVD]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "depth" and "TVD," where "depth" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "TVD." This edge exists because each additional gate layer in a noisy circuit is another opportunity for error to be introduced before the final measurement, so a deeper circuit accumulates more noise by the time it is measured. Based on the "TVD by Transpiled Depth, All Circuits" graph, an increase in "depth" results in a rough increase in "TVD"; for example, an increase of "depth" from 4 to 76 results in an increase in "TVD" from about 0.02 to 0.22. The direction only points from "depth" to "TVD," and not the other way, because "depth" is fixed once the circuit is transpiled, before it is ever run or measured, while "TVD" is only computed afterward, from the measurement results.

- [depth -> success_probability]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "depth" and "success_probability," where "depth" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "success_probability" (applicable only to the Grover circuit). This edge exists for the same reason as "depth -> TVD": more sequential gate layers under a noisy model means more accumulated error by the time the algorithm's amplitude amplification result is measured. Based on the "Grover: Success Probability by Transpiled Depth" graph, as "depth" increases, success probability decreases for the Grover circuit only (the metric does not apply to the other four basic circuits); for example, as "depth" increases from 5 to 65, the probability that the Grover circuit succeeds in finding the target decreases from about 0.9 to 0.27. The direction only points from "depth" to "success_probability," and not the other way, because "depth" is fixed once the circuit is transpiled, before it is run, while "success_probability" is only computed afterward, from the measurement results.

### "count_2q"

- [count_2q -> runtime]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "count_2q" and "runtime," where "count_2q" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "runtime." This edge exists because two-qubit gates are generally more computationally expensive for a simulator to apply than single-qubit gates, since they act on a larger joint state space, so more two-qubit gates mean more of these costlier operations the simulator has to compute, independent of overall circuit depth. Based on the "Runtime by Two-Qubit Gate Count, All Circuits" graph, as the count of two-qubit gates increases (Bell is the only circuit with no two-qubit gates), "runtime" also increases. For example, it is evident from the graph that as the count of two-qubit gates go from 1 to 60, the runtime increase from 0.055 seconds to 0.0102 seconds (approximately). The direction only points from "count_2q" to "runtime," and not the other way, because "count_2q" is fixed once the circuit is transpiled, before it is simulated, while "runtime" is only measured afterward.

- [count_2q -> TVD]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "count_2q" and "TVD," where "count_2q" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "TVD." This edge exists because two-qubit gates are typically noisier and more error-prone in real and simulated quantum hardware than single-qubit gates, so a circuit with more of them accumulates more error before measurement. Based on the "TVD by Two-Qubit Gate Count, All Circuits" graph, as the count of two-qubit gates increases (Bell being an exception, since it has no two-qubit gates), "TVD" also increases. For example, as the count of two-qubit gates go from 1 to 60, the "TVD" increases from 0.02 to 0.22 (approximately). The direction only points from "count_2q" to "TVD," and not the other way, because "count_2q" is fixed once the circuit is transpiled, before it is run, while "TVD" is only computed afterward, from the measurement results.

- [count_2q -> success_probability]: This edge establishes a causal relationship between "count_2q" and "success_probability," where "count_2q" is a variable that affects the outcome metric "success_probability" (applicable only to the Grover circuit). This edge exists for the same reason as "count_2q -> TVD": two-qubit gates, such as the entangling gates used in Grover's oracle and diffusion operator, are more error-prone, so more of them means more accumulated error by the time the marked state is measured. Based on the "Grover: Success Probability by Two-Qubit Gate Count" graph, as the count of two-qubit gates increases, "success_probability" decreases, consistent with the mechanism above. For example, as the count of two-qubit gates go from 2 to 28, the "success_probability" decreases from 0.89 to 0.28 (approximately). The direction only points from "count_2q" to "success_probability," and not the other way, because "count_2q" is fixed once the circuit is transpiled, before it is run, while "success_probability" is only computed afterward, from the measurement results.

## Outcome Metrics

### "runtime"

"runtime" measures the wall-clock time required to simulate a quantum circuit. It is influenced by "num_qubits" directly (a larger qubit count means a larger state space for the simulator to track), and by "depth" and "count_2q," both of which add more sequential or computationally expensive operations for the simulator to process. "circuit_name" and "optimization_level" influence "runtime" only indirectly, by determining the baseline "depth" and "count_2q" of each circuit type, neither has a direct edge to "runtime" of its own. Unlike "TVD" and "success_probability," "runtime" is a purely computational/structural metric; it is not affected by "noise_rate" or "shots," since neither changes how much work the simulator has to do, only how the result is sampled or perturbed afterward.

### "TVD"

"TVD" (total variation distance) measures how far the actual measured probability distribution is from the ideal, noiseless probability distribution. It is the outcome metric with the most incoming causes in this graph, since it is the most general-purpose quality metric, applicable to all five circuit types. "shots" directly affects it through sampling noise (more shots means a more reliable estimate of the true distribution), "noise_rate" affects it directly through environmental/hardware error, and "depth" and "count_2q" affect it by determining how many opportunities exist for that noise to accumulate before measurement. "num_qubits" also has a direct effect on "TVD" beyond what "depth" and "count_2q" already explain; this was confirmed by holding depth and count_2q roughly constant across different qubit counts and still seeing TVD increase, which is consistent with each additional qubit carrying its own idle and readout error that accumulates independently of circuit size. "optimization_level" and "circuit_name" influence "TVD" only indirectly, through the "depth" and "count_2q" mediators.

### "success_probability"

"success_probability" measures the probability that Grover's algorithm successfully amplifies the marked state above the noise floor, and unlike "TVD," it is only defined for the Grover circuit, it has no meaning for the other four circuit types, since they are not search algorithms with a "correct answer" to amplify toward. It shares several of the same incoming causes as "TVD" ("noise_rate," "depth," and "count_2q," for the same accumulated-error reasons), but it also has a direct edge from "circuit_name" that "TVD" does not have, since success specifically depends on Grover's algorithmic structure (its oracle and amplification steps), not just on generic circuit size or gate count.
